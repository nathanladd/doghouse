Course Goals
Students leave with the skills and confidence to handle any AC call in the field — from a complete recovery to diagnosing a subtle voltage-drop failure.
🔄
Understand the Cycle

Walk the refrigerant cycle on a live machine. Understand sensible vs. latent heat, why dry refrigerant matters, and what oil does.

🛠️
Use the Tools

Effortful practice on gauge sets, recovery machines, vacuum pumps, and charging equipment. Every tech leaves having completed a full R&R.

📊
Read Gauge Patterns

Diagnose good, low, overcharged, and airflow-restricted systems from gauge readings and duct temperatures.

⚡
Separate Electrical Failures

Voltage-drop test the AC switch. Bypass pressure switches and clutches. Read MCC flash codes. Don't let a dim LED fool you.

What We're NOT Doing

No quizzes. No tests. No slide-only lectures. Every concept is introduced on the machine and proven by doing. If something goes sideways — good. That's the lesson.

Module 1
Safety First
⏱️ 15–20 min 💬 Discussion
⚠️ Safety Critical

Cover this before anyone touches equipment. These hazards are real and non-obvious to technicians who haven't worked with refrigerants.

Refrigerant Hazards
1
Pressurized System
R-134a pressure tries to escape the system at all times. It can drive oil or debris into eyes and skin, and it can blow fittings apart. Always relieve pressure properly before opening any fitting.
2
Frostbite
R-134a boiling point is −15°F. Liquid refrigerant released on skin or eyes causes instant frostbite. Always wear safety glasses. Never let liquid contact bare skin.
3
Asphyxiation
Refrigerant is heavier than air and displaces oxygen. In a pit, enclosed shop bay, or low area — it can silently pool and suffocate. Never vent refrigerant indoors.
4
Phosgene Gas
R-134a passing through open flame or very high heat can form phosgene-like toxic gas. No welding near AC lines. No open flames when refrigerant may be present.
5
Exploding Containers
Liquid refrigerant in a container expands as it heats. If the container fills completely, it will explode. Never store containers near heat sources or in direct sun.

Module 2
The Refrigerant Cycle
⏱️ 45–60 min 🚜 Live Machine
Instructor Approach

Teach this cycle by walking around the machine with students. Don't lecture it — point to each component, have them touch the pipes, measure the temps. The machine IS the slide deck.

Two Concepts to Anchor Everything
🌡️
Sensible Heat

Heat you can measure with a thermometer. Temperature changes. The refrigerant stays in the same phase (all gas or all liquid).

💧
Latent Heat

Heat absorbed or released during a phase change — liquid to gas or gas to liquid. This is where the real work happens. Temperature doesn't change; state does.

💡 Teaching Tip — The Boiling Water Analogy Water boiling on a stove stays at 212°F no matter how much heat you add — until it's all vaporized. That's latent heat. The evaporator does the same thing with refrigerant at a much lower temperature (inside the cab).
The Cycle — Walk the Machine
1
Compressor — The Pump
Where on machine: Engine bay, belt-driven
What it does: Draws low-pressure vapor from the evaporator and compresses it into high-pressure, high-temperature vapor. It doesn't create cold — it moves heat.
Feel it: The high-side line leaving the compressor should be very hot.
2
Condenser — Dumps Heat Outside
Where on machine: Forward-facing, front of machine
What it does: Hot high-pressure vapor enters. Ambient air carries the heat away. Refrigerant condenses to a liquid. This is where the heat from inside the cab actually leaves the system.
Feel it: Outlet line is warm-hot liquid. Measure condenser inlet vs. outlet air temp.
3
Receiver/Dryer — Filter & Storage
What it does: Stores liquid refrigerant, filters debris, and absorbs moisture with desiccant.
Key rule: Replace the dryer any time the system is opened. Desiccant has a finite capacity — once saturated, moisture circulates and destroys the system.
4
Expansion Valve / Orifice Tube — The Restriction
What it does: Meters refrigerant flow, dropping pressure and temperature rapidly. High-pressure liquid enters — cold low-pressure mixture exits. This is what makes the evaporator cold.
5
Evaporator — Pulls Heat Into the System
Where on machine: Inside the HVAC box in the cab
What it does: Low-pressure refrigerant boils (absorbs latent heat) from the warm cab air blowing across it. Cab air loses its heat to the refrigerant — that's why it feels cold at the duct.
Measure it: Duct temp 45–53°F is normal. Below 40°F possible on a good system.
Why Clean, Dry Refrigerant Matters

Moisture in the system combines with refrigerant to form acids that eat metal components and clog expansion devices. Contamination coats heat transfer surfaces and insulates them — reducing efficiency. Non-condensables (air) take up space in the condenser and raise high-side pressure without doing useful work.

Oil is necessary to lubricate the compressor, but it circulates through the whole system. Too much oil coats heat-transfer surfaces — exactly like contamination. Always account for oil during any service.

Module 4
Tools & Recovery
⏱️ 60–90 min 🚜 Hands-On Every Tech
Instructor Note — Effortful Practice

Every technician completes a full recovery on their own machine. No watching. The 28-minute recovery process is a real wait — use that time to discuss oil accounting, dryer replacement rules, and vacuum principles while watching gauges.

Full Recovery Process
1
Connect the Recovery Machine
Connect to both high-side and low-side service ports. Use the appropriate hose colors. Verify machine is off before connecting.
2
Recover to Reusable Field Bottle
Field techs recover to a reusable portable bottle — not the large shop recovery tank. This is what goes on the service truck.
⏱️ Allow ~28 minutes for complete recovery. Watch for: pressure drops to 0, machine cycles off. Note oil pulled out — this counts toward oil accounting.
3
Oil Accounting
Why does recovery pull different amounts of oil?
  • Oil circulates with refrigerant — amount recovered depends on how much migrated
  • Record oil pulled during recovery
  • Replace only what was removed + what spec calls for new components
  • Effects of too much oil: reduced cooling, possible compressor issues
4
Dehydrate (Pull Vacuum)
Connect vacuum pump. Pull to 29.9 in/Hg minimum. Hold for 30 minutes minimum after pump is shut off — watch for rise (indicates leak or moisture).
Why vacuum pulls moisture: lowering pressure lowers water's boiling point. Moisture boils out of the system.
5
Charge to Spec
Add correct refrigerant weight per service manual for this machine. Charge by weight — not pressure. Pressure varies with temperature. Log charge amount on RO.

Module 5
Reading Pressures
⏱️ 45 min 🚜 Multiple Machines
Instructor Note

Set up machines in different conditions before class (or progressively during): normal, low charge, overcharge, blocked condenser, bagged cab filters. Have students read gauges and predict what's wrong before telling them. Discuss the "why" behind each pattern.

Pressure–Temperature Relationship

Refrigerant pressure and temperature are directly related — they follow the P-T chart. This is the foundation of diagnosis. If you know the pressure, you know the refrigerant temperature at that point in the system. This is how you know if the evaporator is cold enough or the condenser is rejecting heat properly.

Normal Operating Baseline
ParameterNormal Value
Duct (louver) temperature45–53°F
Low-side pressure (approx. 70–80°F ambient)25–45 psi
High-side pressure (approx. 70–80°F ambient)150–250 psi
Compressor housing tempNear engine temp

Module 6
Diagnosis Process
⏱️ 60 min 🚜 Problem-Solving Scenarios
The 7-Step Approach
This is the repeatable mental framework for any AC call. Field expedient: fast, logical, and designed to avoid quoting unnecessary repairs.
1
Verify the Concern
Full engine speed. AC on full cold. Blower on high. Close cab. Run 5 minutes. Measure duct temp. Is this actually a complaint worth diagnosing, or is it working?
2
Inspect the Machine
  • Condenser/coolers: restricted or dirty?
  • Compressor clutch: engaging?
  • Belt: intact and tight?
  • Fresh air and recirculating filters: restricted?
  • Debris behind seat plugging recirculating filter?
  • Blower: noisy or weak?
3
Separate Electrical from Refrigerant
Key on, engine off test: turn AC on. After 6-second MCC delay, clutch should engage. If it doesn't — is it electrical or low pressure? Install gauges to know.
4
Check MCC Flash Codes
MCC codes can point directly to the subsystem. Know the AC-related codes for the models in your fleet. (See Specs & Codes page.)
5
Bypass Failures to Confirm
  • AC Switch: Voltage-drop test. Even a slightly dim LED = corrosion. Bypass and retest.
  • Pressure Switch: Bypass and install gauges to check charge. R-Series: accessible without raising cab.
  • Compressor Clutch: Bypass with high-current test light to verify the rest of the system works before condemning the compressor.
  • Heater Valve: Hot coolant bypass can heat the cab. Clamp heater hose to isolate.
6
Repair per Latest Service Info
Check MyMachine for service letters and knowledge articles before ordering parts. Use correct oil quantity. Replace dryer if system was opened.
7
Verify the Repair
Same as Step 1: 5 minutes, full load, measure duct temp. Document concern, cause, and correction including both verify-concern and verify-repair results.
Voltage-Drop Testing the AC Switch
⚡ Most Common Miss

The LED in the AC switch may only be slightly dim — easy to overlook. A dim LED means the switch is eating voltage through corrosion. That lost voltage means the clutch won't engage reliably. Always voltage-drop test this switch on an AC complaint where the clutch isn't engaging.

Measure voltage drop across the closed switch while the clutch circuit is powered. More than 0.3–0.5V drop = replace the switch. Bypass the switch temporarily to confirm the clutch works — if it does, the switch is the culprit.

Module 7
Machine Exercises
⏱️ Remaining Day 🚜 All Machines
Instructor Note — Problem-Based Learning

Present each exercise as a real dispatch: "This machine came in with a customer complaint of warm air. Figure out what's wrong." Let students work through the 7 steps before revealing the planted fault. Debrief each one — what gauges said, what was confirmed by bypass, what was the first test that pointed you there.

Station Exercises
✅
Station 1 — Baseline: Known Good Machine
Machine 1
  • Record gauge readings at stabilized operating conditions
  • Measure duct temperature, condenser inlet/outlet air temperature
  • Feel pipes: identify hot high-side, cold low-side, frost line at TXV
  • This is your reference for everything else today
Goal: Establish a personal baseline. Know what "right" feels like before diagnosing what's wrong.
❄️
Station 2 — Low Charge
Machine 2
  • Machine is partially discharged to below normal
  • Run through 7-step diagnosis
  • Read and record gauge readings
  • Identify: does the clutch engage? What does the pressure switch do?
  • Partially charge with ~1 lb — "look and listen" to see the system come alive
Goal: Recognize low-charge gauge pattern. Practice using partial charge as a diagnostic tool.
💨
Station 3 — Blocked Condenser
Machine 3
  • Block condenser with cardboard (simulate real-world debris)
  • Compare gauges to baseline and low-charge readings
  • Measure condenser inlet vs. outlet air temp
  • Remove blockage — watch pressures recover in real time
Goal: Distinguish overcharge/restriction from low charge using temp measurements, not just pressures.
🛍️
Station 4 — Blocked Cab Filters
Machine 4
  • Bag recirculating and fresh air filters completely
  • Compare gauge readings — may look like low charge
  • Check duct airflow by feel
  • Measure duct temp — cold air, no volume
Goal: Understand that "warm cab" doesn't always mean refrigerant problem — airflow first.
⚡
Station 5 — Electrical Fault (AC Switch)
Machine 5
  • Machine has a corroded AC switch planted (or simulated)
  • LED is slightly dim — notice it?
  • Key-on engine-off test: does clutch engage after 6-second delay?
  • Voltage-drop test the switch
  • Bypass switch — does clutch engage now?
Goal: Never overlook a dim switch light. Voltage drop is the test — your eyes alone will miss it.
🔄
Station 6 — Full R&R (Every Tech)
All / Rotating
  • Each technician performs a full recovery on their own machine
  • Record oil pulled during recovery
  • Pull vacuum — 30-minute hold
  • Charge to spec by weight
  • Verify repair: 5-minute run, measure duct temp
  • Document C/C/C (concern, cause, correction)
Goal: Complete competence on the full service process. Every tech leaves having done it start to finish.
